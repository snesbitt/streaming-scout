02No metadata — so it gets researched

Netflix and Prime's own history pages disclose a title and a date — nothing else. Every genre tag and cast credit across the full watch-history record was independently sourced through per-title research, not inferred from platform metadata. Watch status is likewise inferred from episode counts and gaps, never presented as scraped fact. Coming Soon draws only from named, verifiable sources — BritBox, PBS Masterpiece, Netflix Tudum, Den of Geek, What's on Netflix, JustWatch — cited so each claim's reliability can be judged independently rather than simply trusted. Where verified art doesn't exist, the record says so plainly by leaving the space empty, rather than substituting a guess or a placeholder that looks like it might be real.

03Signature genres in full, generic at a third

Broad categories — Comedy, Drama — are shared across too many unrelated titles to constitute meaningful evidence on their own. Genres with real recurrence in your own history (British Procedural, Spy Thriller, Mystery, Action Thriller) are weighted in full, with an added signal for cast overlap against titles you've already valued. A genre you've explicitly rejected is capped regardless of any other data pointing the other way — expressed preference overrides inferred pattern.

Full weight

British Procedural, Spy Thriller, Mystery, Action Thriller — genres with real recurrence in your own watch history, plus cast-overlap bonus.

Capped, regardless of score

Sci-fi, fantasy, rom-com — explicitly rejected. A high score elsewhere can't override a genre you've already ruled out.

04A snapshot, not a live connection

This reads from a single published snapshot rather than a live session — nothing touches your Netflix or Prime account while you're using it. Synchronization happens deliberately, in a Cowork session, either at your request or on a fixed Monday schedule, and only then is the static record rebuilt.

05Publishing changes automatically

Every change publishes to the live site automatically, with no manual deploy step. That's what made it possible to add the first fully live feature: removing a title now updates every device immediately, not just the one you dismissed it on.

06What's still manual

Removing a title syncs everywhere instantly. Keeping it off next week's rebuilt list still takes one message to Claude. There's also no automated check that a Monday update actually published — until there is, checking the live site by eye is the control of record.

Two lessons worth knowing

A better formula can't fix a bad starting list. An early version ranked movies from a generic "trending now" list — the same list every visitor to a streaming site sees. A slapstick comedy nearly outscored a spy thriller starring Rami Malek, because both titles came from that same undifferentiated pool and every genre was weighted equally. The fix wasn't a smarter formula — it was pulling candidates only from Susan's own multi-year watch history instead. No amount of scoring sophistication rescues a candidate list that isn't personalized to begin with.

Claude asks before it spends — every time, no exceptions. Registering this site's domain name is the clearest example: before buying streamingscout.org, Claude confirmed the exact domain and the $10.49 price with Susan, and would not complete the purchase without her card on file and her explicit go-ahead. That's not a one-time courtesy — it's the standing rule applied to every dollar this system touches.
